- **What the Fragment Shader is**
	- The **Fragment Shader** (sometimes called **pixel shader**) is a **programmable stage** in Vulkan.
	- It runs **once per fragment** generated by rasterization.
	- Its main job is to compute the **final color and other outputs** of each fragment before it is sent to the framebuffer.
    
      
    
    ---
    
- **Main Purposes of the Fragment Shader**
	- **Color Computation**
		
		- Determines the RGBA color of a fragment.
		- Can be a simple constant color or a complex function involving lighting, shadows, and material properties.
		
	- **Texture Sampling**
		
		- Reads textures (diffuse maps, normal maps, specular maps, etc.) to add detail to surfaces.
		- Uses interpolated texture coordinates from the rasterizer.
		
	- **Lighting & Shading**
		
		- Implements lighting models (Phong, Blinn-Phong, PBR, etc.).
		- Uses interpolated normals, positions, and other attributes.
		
	- **Special Effects**
		
		- Transparency, reflections, refractions, procedural patterns, toon shading, etc.
		
	- **Optional Outputs**
		
		- Can output depth values (for custom depth testing).
		- Can output to multiple render targets (MRTs), e.g., in deferred rendering.
		- Can write custom data like normals, material IDs, etc., to offscreen buffers (G-buffers).
    
      
    
    ---
    

- **What the Fragment Shader does *not***
	- It does **not decide which fragments are visible** (depth/stencil tests handle that later).
	- It does **not blend fragments into the framebuffer** (the output merger does that).
	- It does **not generate fragments** (rasterization does that).
    
      
    
    ---
    
- **Analogy**
    
      
    Think of the fragment shader as the **artist painting each pixel**:  
    
	- Rasterization provides a sketch of which pixels belong to a triangle.
	- The fragment shader fills in the color and shading detail for each one.
    
      
    
    ---
    
- **Examples of Uses**
	- Applying a texture to a 3D model.
	- Implementing per-pixel lighting and shadows.
	- Doing post-processing effects (blur, bloom, HDR, color grading).
	- Rendering special effects like water, fire, or glass.
    
      
    
    ---
    
      
    **In short:**  
      
    The **Fragment Shader in Vulkan** computes the final **color, depth, and other outputs of each fragment**, using interpolated data from earlier stages, textures, and lighting calculations. It’s the stage that makes your geometry look _realistic_ (or stylized).
