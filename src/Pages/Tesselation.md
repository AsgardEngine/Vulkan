### What Tessellation is in Vulkan

The **Tessellation stage** in Vulkan is an **optional, programmable stage** of the graphics pipeline that sits **between the Vertex Shader and the Geometry Shader (or directly before Rasterization if no geometry shader is used)**.  
  
Its purpose is to **take coarse geometry (usually patches defined by a few control points)** and **subdivide (tessellate) it into finer pieces**, producing more detailed geometry dynamically on the GPU.  

### Why Tessellation is Useful

- **Level of Detail (LOD):**  
	You can tessellate more when the object is close to the camera and less when it’s far, saving performance.  
    
- **Smooth Surfaces:**
    Instead of sending thousands of vertices from the CPU, you can send just a few control points and let the GPU tessellate into a smooth surface (e.g., curved terrain, spheres, characters).  
    
- **Dynamic Detail:**
    Enables adaptive subdivision of geometry at runtime without changing your input mesh.  


## How Tessellation Works in Vulkan

The tessellation stage is actually split into **three parts**:  

- **Tessellation Control Shader (TCS)**
	
	- Input: patches (groups of vertices, instead of single vertices).
	- Purpose:
		
		- Controls **how much tessellation** should happen (inner/outer tessellation levels).
		- Can modify or generate new control point data.
		
	- Output: tessellation levels + control point data for the next stage.

- **Fixed-Function Tessellator** (non-programmable)
	
	- Uses tessellation levels provided by the TCS.
	- Subdivides the patch into smaller primitives (triangles, quads, or isolines).
	- Produces a dense set of coordinates (barycentric/UV inside the patch).
	
- **Tessellation Evaluation Shader (TES)**
	
	- Runs once per tessellated vertex generated by the fixed-function tessellator.
	- Input: barycentric/UV coordinates within the patch.
	- Purpose: computes the **final position** (and other attributes) of each tessellated vertex, often evaluating a curved surface or displacement map.



## Example Uses of Tessellation

- Terrain rendering (with adaptive tessellation based on distance).
- Water surfaces (with dynamic wave detail).
- Characters (smooth muscles/skin detail without high-poly meshes).
- Curved objects (cylinders, spheres, cars, etc.).
- Displacement mapping (tessellate a flat mesh and push vertices using a height map).


## Analogy

Think of tessellation like **zooming into a digital map**:  

- At a far zoom, you just see a few coarse shapes.
- As you zoom in, the tessellation system dynamically adds more detail (roads, rivers, etc.) — you didn’t need to store all that detail upfront.

---

Tessellation in Vulkan allows the GPU to **subdivide coarse geometry into finer geometry on the fly**, controlled by shaders. It enables **smooth curves, displacement, and adaptive level of detail**, saving bandwidth while increasing realism.
